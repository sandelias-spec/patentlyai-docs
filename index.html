How PatentlyAI Reasons

A stage-by-stage account of what each agent is actually instructed to determine, in what order, and what stops it from making things up — across both the Office Action Response pipeline and the Claim Generator pipeline.

Written for readers who already know what a 35 U.S.C. §101/103/112 rejection is. Covers the current pipelines end to end, including per-citation verdict detail at Checkpoint 3, dedicated 101 and 112(b) response strategies, and the key-free Google Patents connectors used for reference retrieval and prior-art search.

Synthesis
Where the guardrails live

Both pipelines are built the same way underneath: a chain of narrowly-scoped LLM calls, each fed only what it needs and told explicitly what it may not invent, interleaved with human checkpoints that are never rendered as approve-by-default. Four patterns recur enough to name up front — they explain why a given stage is shaped the way it is, more than any individual stage description will.

  • 01
    Model tier follows legal stakes, not stage order. Mechanical extraction (spec analysis, structural parsing) runs on Sonnet. Anything requiring genuine legal judgment — inventive-concept framing, combination-attack construction, eligibility and definiteness calls — runs on Opus, and the higher-stakes half of those also pay for extended thinking.
  • 02
    Checkpoints ask a real question, not a formality. Several defaults are deliberately absent: Checkpoint 2 in both workflows proposes nothing and forces the user to set claim strategy from scratch; the OA workflow's final checkpoint imposes a 15-minute cooldown before approval is even offered, because "a real read of a USPTO response takes more than 15 minutes."
  • 03
    Grounding rules are written as prohibitions, not aspirations. Nearly every agent's prompt states what it must not do — invent a spec passage, invent hardware to rescue an eligibility argument, invent a citation — and several of those prohibitions are additionally enforced in code after the model call returns, not just requested in the prompt.
  • 04
    Extended thinking buys judgment at the cost of determinism. On the current model family, extended thinking forces temperature 1 with no lower-variance option. For the agents making binding legal calls (101 eligibility, 112(b) definiteness) this produces documented run-to-run swings — the mitigation is independent resampling and agreement-checking, not a single trusted pass.

I. Office Action Response

Takes a mailed Office Action plus the pending claims and produces a filed-ready response: a claim listing that satisfies 37 CFR 1.121, and an argument section addressed to every rejection. Structural extraction

Stage 1
Sonnet 5

Reads the Office Action and pulls out every rejection (statutory basis, claims affected, references cited, the examiner's rationale near-verbatim), the cited references themselves, and — when present — the OA's own "Claim Interpretation" section, copied rather than paraphrased, since Argument Strength will need it verbatim later to challenge a specific claim-construction position.

Grounding rule

The agent is explicitly told it does not make legal judgments here — it identifies what the examiner said and how the document is organized, nothing more. Missing fields are left null rather than guessed (no examiner name on the document means no examiner name in the output), and the statutory basis is canonicalized to a fixed vocabulary rather than left as free text the rest of the pipeline would have to re-interpret.

One field is deliberately never trusted to the model: if the extracted application number disagrees with the number the user already supplied when starting the run, the orchestrator silently overwrites the model's answer. Checkpoint 1 is the one chance a human gets to catch an extraction error on everything else, so the one fact that's already known with certainty doesn't need to survive that review intact by luck.

Checkpoint 1

Does this match the actual Office Action?

The reviewer sees the parsed header, the rejections table, and the cited references list, and is asked exactly that question — not "does this look reasonable," but does it match. Editing drops into a JSON editor for hand-correction, re-validated against the schema before the next review.

Inventive concept

Stage 2
Opus 4.8Extended thinking · 12K

Called, in the prompt's own words, the single most important reasoning step in the workflow — getting the inventive concept wrong here propagates through mapping, strategy, and drafting. Given the pending claims, spec excerpts, and the newly-cited rejections, the agent must produce three genuinely distinct re-framings of what's actually inventive, not three phrasings of the same idea:

Three required framings
FramingCharacter
NarrowTightly bound to disclosed embodiments — most defensible, narrowest resulting scope.
BalancedThe framing most likely to survive examination while still preserving meaningful scope.
ExpansiveThe broadest framing the specification can plausibly support — highest risk of a continued rejection or a new 112 problem.

Each framing carries its own distinguishing elements, an honest risk list, and specification-support citations. The estoppel-avoidance habit that recurs everywhere downstream starts here: the agent is told never to write "the present invention," since that phrase reads later in prosecution as a binding characterization of the whole disclosure — "the claimed invention" leaves room to argue a broader position later.

Grounding rule

Every distinguishing element must trace to something the applicant actually disclosed. The risk section is required to be honest even when that's inconvenient — if a framing has a real 112 exposure problem, the agent is told to say so rather than sell the framing.

Checkpoint 2

Which framing becomes the spine of the response?

Every downstream agent's characterization of what's novel is constrained by this choice, which is why the checkpoint is framed as the most strategically important decision in the workflow rather than a rubber stamp. A Custom option exists for a hand-written statement and element list that bypasses the model's three proposals entirely.

Prior art mapping

Stage 3
Sonnet 5 → Opus 4.8Escalates to thinking on ambiguity

Runs in two passes. Pass one dispatches one independent Sonnet call per (limitation, reference) pair — deliberately isolated from every other limitation, to prevent context drift where an agent's read of limitation 3 quietly leans on how it just read limitation 2. Pass two re-runs, on Opus with extended thinking, only the pairs that came back AMBIGUOUS or DEBATABLE in pass one, with permission to deliberate longer and an instruction to either resolve the ambiguity or state precisely what evidence would.

Level 1 disclosure verdict (every rejection)
VerdictMeaning
DisclosedEvery element of the limitation is explicitly taught by the reference.
Not disclosedAt least one element is clearly absent.
AmbiguousThe reference's teaching is genuinely unclear — escalated to pass two.
DebatableThe examiner could argue disclosure exists, but a reasonable counter-argument that it doesn't also exists.

An implied-but-not-explicit element is capped at DEBATABLE — never promoted to DISCLOSED — and "inherent disclosure" requires the missing element be necessarily present in every instance the reference describes, not merely possibly present in some.

103-ONLY · LEVEL 2

Combination analysis

A §103 rejection concedes that no single embodiment discloses everything; the examiner's case instead rests on a POSITA supposedly combining or modifying disclosed elements to reach the claim — which means the Level 1 verdict alone answers the wrong question for these rejections. Three additional fields are required only here: the examiner's combination theory restated accurately (with an explicit instruction not to build a strawman — "if elements genuinely appear in scattered form, say so"); the gap between what the examiner asserts a cited passage teaches and what it actually says, quoted; and the combination's vulnerability, grounded in the passage text across five recognized attack grounds — different problem solved, different principle of operation, teaching away, hindsight reconstruction, and unpredictable result.

Grounding rule

"If the examiner's reading is accurate, say so; a false gap claim destroys credibility." The same honesty requirement applies to the combination-vulnerability field — if the rationale for combining actually holds up, the agent is told to admit that rather than manufacture an attack.

No separate verification pass exists yet beyond this two-pass structure — a citation-verifier hook in the orchestrator is currently a stub.

Argument strength evaluation

Stage 4
Opus 4.8Extended thinking · 16K

The prompt casts the agent explicitly as senior prosecution counsel thinking about the examiner's burden and what will actually move the case toward allowance, not slogans. It runs a fixed sequence of steps per rejection; several of the highest-leverage decisions are made deterministically in code around the call rather than left to the model to notice on its own.

STEP 1

Classify the rejection's architecture

Five possible shapes, each dictating a different argument structure: single-reference anticipation (element-by-element non-disclosure is dispositive); single-reference multi-embodiment §103 (the examiner already concedes scattered disclosure — arguing non-disclosure answers the wrong question, so the combination gets attacked first); multi-reference §103 (adds reference-incompatibility arguments); official-notice-based (a formal MPEP 2144.03(C) traverse); and the two statutory bases with dedicated architectures below. For the last two, classification is not left to the model's own judgment — a directive line computed in code from the statutory basis already extracted at Stage 1 tells the model definitively when §101 or §112(b) applies, rather than asking it to infer the classification itself.

STEP 2 · §103 ONLY

Combination attack

Grounds explicitly in KSR v. Teleflex and MPEP 2142–2143: the examiner bears the burden of articulated reasoning with rational underpinning. Builds four arguments from the mapper's Level 2 fields — a motivation critique where the stated rationale is missing or conclusory, a predictability critique grounded in the reference's actual design goals rather than the claimed invention's vocabulary, teaching-away evidence limited to passages that actually discourage the claimed path (an empty list is treated as an honest answer, not a failure), and a hindsight argument where the only roadmap to the combination is the applicant's own claim language.

STEP 2B · §101 ONLY current

Eligibility — Alice/Mayo

Applies the two-step framework with an explicit reminder that Alice/Mayo is the name of the test, not a precedent to cite for its holding — no case law is named anywhere in this argument. Step 1 restates the examiner's own directed-to characterization accurately (not the applicant's preferred spin), then challenges it on the claim's character as a whole: a practical application of an abstract idea is not directed to the abstract idea itself. Step 2 identifies specific claim limitations providing "significantly more," each tied to a named specification paragraph.

STEP 2C · §112(b) ONLY current

Indefiniteness

Applies the MPEP 2173.05 reasonable-certainty standard, cited by section rather than by case name. Identifies the disputed term and the examiner's reading, then makes a binary call — is the term genuinely ambiguous, meaning an amendment is needed, or is there already a reasonable definite construction sitting in the specification, meaning the argument alone should suffice? A clarifying amendment is only proposed in the first case, and is required to narrow no more than necessary.

Grounding rule · both 2B and 2C

"Never invent a technical mechanism the specification does not disclose — an unsupported eligibility argument is worse than none." Every anchor for an eligibility or indefiniteness argument must trace to material actually provided; if none exists, the agent states that explicitly rather than presenting an unsupported fix as available.

STEP 3–7

Claim construction, amendments, interview strategy, official notice, path recommendation

Flags where the examiner reads a narrower claim term at too high a level of generality, and separately — only when the OA carries an explicit Claim Interpretation section — challenges an examiner treating a positively recited method step as merely optional (citing MPEP 2111.04, since such a step cannot be read out under broadest-reasonable-interpretation). Proposed amendments require a genuine structural distinction and a mandatory specification citation: "if you cannot cite support, do not propose it; an unsupported amendment invites a §112(a) rejection." A deterministic cross-check (not an LLM judgment) compares every limitation the rationale purports to reject against the actual current claim text, flagging where the examiner has imported language from a different claim or embodiment. Dependent claims each get judged on their own added limitation's merits, independent of the parent's fate — genuinely strong ones get an arguendo "independent allowability" argument, with an explicit instruction not to manufacture one for a dependent the art plainly reaches anyway. Pre-emptive counter-arguments are populated only where a rebuttal is genuinely probable, both fields together or neither — "padding the response with refutations of objections the examiner would never raise is a defect, not a feature." The final step scores ARGUE_ONLY / AMEND_AND_ARGUE / INTERVIEW_FIRST against each other by success probability, with a hard rule that a Final OA never recommends argue-only unless the rejection's strength is rated STRONG.

Two corrections happen in code after the call returns, not inside the prompt: the recommended strategy is overwritten if it drifted from what the recommended path actually implies, and the full set of examiner-cited paragraph numbers per rejection is computed by regex over the rationale — becoming the mandatory coverage checklist Stage 5 must satisfy.

Checkpoint 3

Which references matter, and does the mapping hold up?

Shows each rejection's classification, recommended path with its success probability, and rationale, with the option to override the recommended strategy across any of eight procedural vehicles. current A per-rejection citation detail view sits alongside it — not a new model call, but a direct reshaping of Stage 3's already-computed verdicts: for every reference cited in a rejection, it surfaces each mapped limitation's verdict and the strongest available quote (the examiner-assertion-vs-passage gap where one exists, falling back to the supporting passage text, then to the raw reasoning). It's what lets a reviewer see, reference by reference and limitation by limitation, exactly which of the examiner's specific citations the mapper flagged as an overreach — before spending any time on the drafted prose. Any reference whose text still couldn't be resolved (no upload, no auto-fetch match) is surfaced here as a named warning rather than silently absent from the argument.

Reference retrieval current

The examiner cites references the applicant hasn't necessarily uploaded. When a citation can't be matched to an uploaded file, the pipeline fetches it directly from Google Patents — no API key, no search step: a granted patent or published application's claims and description sit at a URL built directly from its number. The fetcher tries a bounded list of plausible kind-code guesses when the extracted identifier is missing one, fails silently per reference rather than aborting the run, and is capped to five references per run (prioritized to the ones actually applied in a rejection), since fetching every cited reference on an OA that lists thirty could overflow the drafter's output budget on its own. Whatever it retrieves is treated identically to a manually uploaded reference from that point forward — sliced down to the examiner-cited paragraph windows before reaching the mapper.

Drafting

Stage 5
Sonnet 5, promoted to Opus 4.8 on a Final OA or a flagged borderline rejectionThinking on promotion · 12K

Produces two internally consistent views in one call — the filed prose and a structured argument record used for estoppel tracking on the next Office Action — after first consulting the prosecution history's estoppel warnings. Thirteen hard rules govern the output; a few carry real teeth beyond a prompt instruction:

Selected hard rules
RuleWhat it prevents
No invented citationsOnly passages present in the verified mapping may be cited.
Banned phrasing"the present invention," "Applicant admits," absolute words about the prior art — language that narrows position in a later dispute.
Full claim listingEvery pending claim appears with the correct 37 CFR 1.121 status label; text is never fabricated for a claim it wasn't given.
Inverted claim chartEvery §103 argument carries one row per examiner-mapped limitation, in the examiner's own order, before any prose elaboration.
Paragraph coverageEvery examiner-cited paragraph number must be explicitly addressed somewhere in the response.
Code-enforced, not just requested

Paragraph coverage is checked by regex after the draft comes back; if any required paragraph number is missing from the text, the orchestrator automatically re-invokes the drafter exactly once with a correction block naming what's missing — the only place in this workflow where the code retries a model call on its own rather than surfacing the gap to a human.

The mandatory §103 argument order mirrors the legal logic directly: frame the examiner's actual combination theory first (conceding scattered disclosure where that's true, never opening with a §102-style "does not disclose"), attack the combination under KSR, engage every cited paragraph individually, raise genuine-absence arguments only afterward for elements truly missing from the entire reference, and give every dependent claim its own paragraph rather than a generic "allowable by dependency" rollup. Per-statutory-basis, the architecture branches the same way Stage 4 classified it: §101 writes the two-step rebuttal from the eligibility argument with no case law named; §112(b) writes a definiteness rebuttal or a targeted amendment from the indefiniteness argument, not a prior-art-shaped response.

Checkpoint 4

Final review, with a floor on how fast that can happen

Shows the full claim listing with an amendment diff and every argument section, plus a generated Word preview. A 900-second cooldown is enforced before approval is even offered — the reasoning is stated directly in the code: a real read of a USPTO response takes more than fifteen minutes, and if the user could approve faster than that, they didn't read it. The default keystroke is abort, not approve, so silence or a mis-keyed input can't accidentally file anything. Separately, the workflow hard-aborts before this checkpoint is even reached if the claim listing itself fails 37 CFR 1.121 validation, rather than showing a human a document that was never going to be filable.

Spec analysis & source identity

Stages 1 – 1b
Sonnet 5

Spec analysis is deliberately mechanical — technical field, problem addressed, solution disclosed, embodiments with their paragraph/figure locations, technical advantages, key terms, and a handful of candidate CPC codes to seed the prior-art search later. It's explicitly told not to interpret novelty or judge patentability, and to return a short or empty list rather than pad one out if the disclosure genuinely doesn't support more.

Source identity, run alongside it, extracts only what's explicitly stated on the cover page and tail of the document — assignee, inventors, and every priority application number verbatim — and self-rates its own confidence from HIGH down to NONE rather than asserting a guess. It's wrapped in a try/except at the orchestrator level and falls back to NONE on any failure, since its only downstream purpose is feeding the family-screen classifier at Stage 3b, not blocking the run.

Checkpoint 2

Claim strategy — set by the human, proposed by no one

No agent proposes a default here. The reviewer sets the number of independent claims, each one's claim type (method / system / device / apparatus / computer-readable medium / composition / kit / use), a breadth target, a prosecution posture (defensive, offensive, or balanced), and a dependents-per-independent target. Framed in the code itself as the most important strategic decision in the workflow, precisely because nothing pre-fills it — the user is forced to engage rather than confirm a suggestion.

Prior art reconnaissance

Stage 3
Sonnet 5

Four internal steps: a search-plan call proposes six to twelve targeted queries across whichever connectors are actually available; deterministic code executes each query and isolates any single connector's failure from the rest of the plan; results are deduplicated by normalized identifier; a second call assesses every surviving candidate's proximity to the inventive concept.

Proximity assessment
RatingMeaning
Very closeAnticipates the concept or all distinguishing elements — cannot be designed around without abandoning core novelty.
CloseDiscloses most distinguishing elements — needs a careful design-around or an amendment.
ModerateDiscloses some elements; meaningful distance remains.
DistantTangentially related; listed for completeness, unlikely to be cited by an examiner.
Grounding rule

The assessor may only use the record information it was given — it cannot invent what a reference discloses beyond its abstract. A short abstract that doesn't support a confident call defaults to Moderate with an explicit note why, rather than a confident guess in either direction. If every connector query in a plan errors out, the coverage assessment is overridden in code to an explicit search-failed message rather than trusting whatever the model's own phrasing happened to produce — a failed search is never allowed to read as "no prior art found."

Three search sources current

Lens.org — structured keyword/CPC/assignee search, requires a paid registered token. patent_mcp_server — full-text search against USPTO's own PPUBS and PatentsView databases through a local process; the default-required source since it's entirely free once configured. Google Patents — a connector against Google's own public search endpoint, needing no API key or registration at all, added specifically to give a user with neither a Lens token nor working USPTO credentials a real search rather than an empty one. It paces itself to one request every three seconds per run and trips a circuit breaker the moment it's rate-limited — every further call on that run fails immediately with no additional request sent, so a block on this one source never cascades into starving the rest of the search plan of its query budget.

Family & ODP screen

Stage 3b
Deterministic — no LLM call

A reference search can correctly surface a document that isn't actually adversarial prior art at all — it might be the applicant's own earlier filing, governed by obviousness-type double patenting and curable with a terminal disclaimer rather than an argument over novelty. Assignee name alone is not a reliable signal across a filing's life (the code cites a real motivating case where a company's name changed entirely between related filings), so the classifier is deliberately built as a pure, LLM-free function with a fixed precedence: a shared priority or continuity application number outweighs everything; two or more shared inventors is treated as equally strong; a single shared inventor plus a matching assignee is treated as strong, a single shared inventor alone as merely possible; assignee overlap by itself is never enough on its own for a firm same-family call.

Grounding rule

Whenever there's no source identity to compare against, or no enrichment data came back, the verdict is UNKNOWN — never a confident guess in either direction. The classifier is explicitly designed this way so the recon LLM is never the one making this determination.

Checkpoint 3

Which references does the drafter need to design around?

Every reference is shown with its proximity rating, family label, and characterization; the default priority set is every Very-Close/Close reference minus anything flagged same-family, editable by hand against the known identifier list.

Claim drafting

Stage 4
Opus 4.8

Promoted from Sonnet after a review found real drafting-strategy-adherence gaps — disjunctive "at least one of" language surfacing in claims where the strategy had called for a required combination. Drafting is conditioned on the chosen breadth and posture (narrow claims stack several specific limitations; expansive claims recite only the core concept and flag the resulting 112/103 risk explicitly in notes), and rests every independent claim on a genuinely novel combination of disclosed mechanisms rather than a single feature that could be conventional on its own.

Grounding rule

Explicitly forbidden from adding undisclosed hardware or security mechanisms — a secure element, a signed token, a nonce — just to chase a stronger eligibility posture: "doing so trades a §101 problem for a §112(a) new-matter problem." In apparatus/system/device/medium claims, an external actor's actions (a remote server, a counterparty) may only be recited as intended use, never as a positive method step, to avoid a mixed-statutory-class defect.

Support, definiteness & eligibility validators

Stages 5 · 5b · 6
Opus 4.85b & 6 use extended thinking · 12K

Three independent checks run after every draft, initial or refined.

STAGE 5 · §112(a)

Written-description support

RiskMeaning
No riskExplicit disclosure, ideally in more than one place.
Weak supportA single passage or an implication — survivable, but an examiner may probe it.
Missing supportA blocking issue: the claim cannot be prosecuted as-is.

Implicit support only counts when the specification's broader disclosure makes a limitation necessarily implied, and the inference chain has to be documented, not asserted. Never invents spec content: an unsupported limitation gets an empty passage list and a Missing verdict rather than a fabricated citation.

STAGE 5B · §112(b)

Definiteness

Explicitly out of scope here: whether something is disclosed at all — that's Stage 5's job. This stage assumes full disclosure and asks only whether the claim language is clear and of proper statutory scope, across five defect types, each carrying its own severity and, independently, how mechanical or judgment-laden the fix is:

DefectWhat it flags
No antecedent basisA "the X" / "said X" with no earlier "a X" to trace back to, in the claim or a parent it depends from.
IndefiniteTerms of degree with no measurement standard, ambiguous references, unascertainable functional language.
Improper mixed classAn apparatus/system/device claim reciting method steps, or an external actor's actions, as positive elements.
Dependent scopeA dependent that broadens, contradicts, or fails to narrow its parent.
Ungrounded terminologyA coined claim term with no textual basis anywhere in the spec, under any phrasing — opt-in, only checked when spec text is supplied.
Grounding rule

"FLAG and ADVISE; do NOT rewrite the entire claim." For ungrounded terminology specifically, the model is told to actively search for the underlying concept under different wording before flagging it — only genuine absence, not just an unfamiliar word choice, counts.

STAGE 6 · §101

Eligibility — Alice/Mayo

Step 1 asks whether the claim's character as a whole is directed to an abstract idea, a law of nature, or a natural phenomenon — explicitly not "does the claim involve one somewhere," since almost every claim touches a mental step or a natural relationship at some level of abstraction; a practical application of an idea is not the idea itself. If Step 1 finds no ineligible category, Step 2 is skipped outright. Step 2 looks for an inventive concept beyond the exception — improvements to how a computer or system actually functions, a specific technical result, an unconventional combination of known elements — versus its absence: generic implementation, well-understood-routine-conventional activity, a bare field-of-use limitation.

Verdict and risk are tracked on two separate axes on purpose — a claim can be a comfortable Eligible call that's nonetheless rated only Medium risk, or a genuinely Borderline call the model still rates Low risk because the margin, while contestable, is wide. For any Borderline-or-worse claim, exactly one concrete reword is proposed, anchored only to material in a disclosure summary built from the spec analysis — if no disclosed anchor exists for a fix, the agent says so explicitly rather than presenting an invented one as available. The same "never invent hardware to rescue eligibility" prohibition from Stage 4 applies here to the proposed reword itself.

Why these three run repeatedly, not once

Extended thinking on the current model family forces temperature 1 with no lower-variance setting. On identical inputs, the definiteness check has been observed to swing from zero to four blocking findings across otherwise-identical reruns, and the eligibility check from zero to twenty high-or-critical-risk claims out of twenty. Both are treated as too consequential to trust to a single pass: they're run several times and reconciled by agreement rather than accepted on the first answer.

Checkpoint 4

Iterative refinement loop

Not a single approval but a loop, capped at ten rounds: every claim is shown with its inline 112(a)/112(b)/101 scores, the reviewer either approves-and-finalizes or picks one or more claims and writes a free-text instruction for each, and the drafter revises.

One rule is enforced twice — once as an instruction, once as code that doesn't trust the instruction. The drafter is told to leave every claim it wasn't asked to touch byte-identical; after every refine call, the orchestrator additionally force-replaces any untouched claim number with its exact prior version, regardless of what the model actually returned, so "only touch what was targeted" is a guarantee rather than a request.

EVERY ROUND

The 112(a) regression gate

After a refine call, only the claims that round could plausibly have affected are re-checked for written-description support — re-deriving a verdict for an untouched, byte-identical claim would just re-confirm what's already known. The gate then compares this round's findings against the round's own starting point: a claim that was already missing support before this round is allowed to remain so — chipping away at pre-existing issues is the loop's normal job — but a claim that becomes newly unsupported by this round's edit is treated as a regression. When one is detected, the orchestrator automatically builds a corrective instruction quoting the newly-unsupported fragment and re-invokes the drafter, up to two bounded retries, before surfacing whatever remains to the human as an explicit warning rather than silently accepting it.

Why only 112(a) gets an automatic retry

A new-matter regression can be mechanically identified and stripped back out. A shift in 112(b) definiteness or 101 eligibility posture cannot be auto-corrected the same way — both require genuine drafting judgment, so they get incremental re-validation and a round-over-round advisory warning (explicitly non-blocking, and explicit that the numbers can move on identical claims from model variance alone) instead of an automatic corrective loop.

Finalizing is a soft gate, not a hard block: if any independent claim sits at Likely-Ineligible or High/Critical 101 risk, approval requires one extra explicit "finalize anyway?" confirmation rather than either silently finalizing or refusing outright — because, as the code puts it, a bad 101 posture can't be safely auto-corrected; it needs real drafting judgment a gate can't supply on its own.

Two features, same source, different jobs

It's easy to conflate these because they hit the same domain: the OA workflow's Google Patents step retrieves the full text of a reference the examiner already named, by number, when it wasn't uploaded. The Claim Generator's Google Patents connector searches for candidate references that were never named at all, by keyword, CPC code, or assignee. One answers "what does this specific document say"; the other answers "what documents exist that I don't know about yet."

Reflects the current state of both pipelines. Superseded implementations referenced only in code comments as history — the PPUBS-based reference lookup replaced by the Google Patents fetch, for instance — are omitted here as no longer part of how either workflow runs.
